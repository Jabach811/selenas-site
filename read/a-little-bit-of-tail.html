A Little Bit of Tail · Book One

Chapter One

The porch light was still on when she pulled into the gravel drive, the way it always was, the way it had been every night of her growing up. Mama said a lit porch told the world somebody was still waiting on you. Tonight it just looked like one more bill nobody had thought to pay.

She sat in the car a minute with the engine ticking and the radio low, working up to it. There is a particular kind of tired that sleep won't touch, and she had been carrying it the whole drive down from the city, mile after mile of dark highway and her own thoughts for company.

"You going to come in," Mama called from the screen door, "or you going to sit out there and let the moths have you?" She was wiping her hands on a dish towel, and even from here her smile was the realest thing in three hundred miles.

Inside, the house smelled like cornbread and coffee and something underneath it she couldn't name but knew by heart. The same clock ticked on the same wall. The same chair waited at the same table. Some places hold still on purpose, just so you have something to come back to.

"Sit," Mama said. "Eat. Then you can tell me what's got your shoulders up around your ears." And because there was nothing on earth left to do but obey, she sat, and she ate, and the telling started the way it always does — slow, and then all at once.
