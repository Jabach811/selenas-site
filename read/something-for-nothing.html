Something for Nothing · Book Three

Chapter One

Nobody hands you anything for free, her grandmother used to say, and if they do, you'd best count your fingers after the handshake. She had spent the better part of thirty years proving the woman right, and the rest of it wishing she hadn't.

So when the letter came — heavy paper, real ink, a lawyer's name embossed at the top like it mattered — her first thought wasn't joy. It was the same thing it always was. What's the catch, and how much is it going to cost me that the price tag doesn't mention.

She read it twice standing up in the kitchen, then a third time sitting down, because by then her knees had opinions of their own. The words didn't change. They just kept saying the impossible thing, plain as day, daring her to believe it.

Outside, the morning went on like mornings do — a dog barking two yards over, somebody's sprinkler ticking against the fence, the world entirely unbothered by the fact that hers had just tilted off its axis.

"Well," she said out loud, to nobody, to the empty kitchen and the lit-up letter and the grandmother who wasn't there to count her fingers for her. "Well. Let's just see what it wants from me." And she reached for the phone.
